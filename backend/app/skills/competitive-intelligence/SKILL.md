# Competitive Intelligence

Build competitor analysis from verifiable sources, explicit confidence levels, and structured comparisons. Competitive intelligence should clarify market choices without inventing competitor-specific facts.

---

## Overview

Competitive intelligence answers who competes, how they win, where they are vulnerable, and what the client should do in response. The work is only useful if competitor claims are sourced and comparable across the roster.

The core principle: never invent competitor-specific claims. If a competitor's pricing, feature, customer, channel, or positioning cannot be sourced, mark it unknown or infer cautiously from disclosed evidence.

Competitive analysis should distinguish facts from interpretation. A pricing page is a fact source. A conclusion about price aggressiveness is an interpretation that should cite the pricing evidence and explain the benchmark.

---

## When to Use

Use competitive intelligence when the engagement requires:

- Market entry competitor mapping.
- Pricing and packaging comparison.
- Product capability benchmarking.
- Positioning and messaging assessment.
- Channel strategy analysis.
- Due diligence on competitive threats.
- Sales enablement battlecards.
- Red team review of strategy assumptions.

Do not use competitive intelligence to fill gaps with plausible-sounding details. Unknown is an acceptable answer when evidence is unavailable.

---

## Core Framework

### Competitor Roster Construction

Build the roster in tiers:

- **Direct competitors**: solve the same customer problem with similar products.
- **Adjacent competitors**: solve the problem through a different workflow, category, or business model.
- **Substitutes**: manual process, internal build, spreadsheet, agency, or incumbent system.
- **Emerging threats**: new entrants, platform expansion, AI-native tools, or regulatory-enabled alternatives.

For each competitor, document why it belongs on the roster. Do not include a company solely because it appears in a search result.

### Comparison Dimensions

| Dimension | Questions to Answer |
|---|---|
| Positioning | Who do they target and what promise do they lead with? |
| Product | What capabilities, integrations, and workflows are visible? |
| Pricing | What packaging, price metric, discounting, or free tier is disclosed? |
| Channel | Do they sell self-serve, inside sales, enterprise sales, partners, or marketplaces? |
| Customer segment | Which industries, company sizes, roles, or use cases are named? |
| Proof points | What customers, case studies, reviews, certifications, or awards are cited? |
| Business model | Subscription, usage, services, transaction, hardware, or hybrid? |
| Momentum | Hiring, funding, launches, partnerships, traffic, or public revenue indicators? |

## Source Hierarchy

Prefer sources in this order:

1. Competitor primary sources: official website, pricing page, docs, filings, app marketplace listing, press releases.
2. Customer-facing evidence: case studies, reviews, procurement listings, app store pages, community forums.
3. Official third-party sources: regulators, government tenders, court records, patent filings.
4. Reputable secondary sources: analyst reports, credible news, investor materials, conference transcripts.
5. Weak secondary sources: blogs, SEO listicles, unverifiable comparison pages.
6. Search snippets or AI summaries: use only as leads, not citations.

Primary sources are not always unbiased, but they are strongest for what a competitor claims about itself. Secondary sources can be stronger for independent validation or criticism.

## Workflow

### Step 1: Define the Competitive Arena

State the customer problem, buying center, geography, product category, and decision context.

Example:

"Competitors for mid-market US clinics buying compliance workflow software in 2026."

This prevents mixing enterprise platforms, consumer apps, and unrelated point solutions.

### Step 2: Build the Initial Roster

Use multiple discovery paths:

- Client-named competitors.
- Search queries around the customer problem and category.
- Review sites and marketplaces.
- Analyst landscapes.
- Customer case studies and procurement records.
- Competitor comparison pages, treated as leads.

Then classify each candidate as direct, adjacent, substitute, or out of scope.

### Step 3: Collect Evidence by Dimension

Create a source-backed comparison table. For every competitor and dimension, record the source, date, and confidence.

Use "not found" instead of guessing. A blank cell with a confidence note is better than fabricated parity.

### Step 4: Normalize for Comparability

Competitors describe themselves differently. Normalize carefully:

- Convert pricing to the same period and unit when possible.
- Separate list price from estimated discounting.
- Distinguish packaged features from add-ons.
- Separate customer claims from named customer evidence.
- Compare channels by observed buying path, not slogans.

### Step 5: Assess Confidence

Use confidence labels:

- **High**: primary source or multiple independent sources agree.
- **Medium**: credible secondary source or indirect primary evidence.
- **Low**: weak source, stale source, or inferred from limited evidence.
- **Unknown**: no reliable evidence found.

Do not hide low-confidence cells. They tell the team where more research is needed.

### Step 6: Synthesize Strategic Implications

Translate facts into implications:

- Where is the market converging?
- Which positions are crowded?
- Which customer segments are underserved?
- Where does the client have credible differentiation?
- Which competitor moves would change the recommendation?

Tie every implication back to observed evidence.

## Evidence Requirements

Every competitor-specific claim should include:

- Competitor name exactly as sourced.
- Source title and URL or source ID.
- Source type: primary, customer-facing, official third-party, secondary, or weak lead.
- Date published or accessed.
- Claim supported by the source.
- Confidence level.

Use primary sources for pricing, product claims, packaging, official partnerships, and stated positioning whenever possible.

Use secondary sources cautiously for revenue estimates, market share, customer traction, and strategic commentary.

---

## Fabrication Red Flags

Stop and verify if you see:

- A competitor-specific price with no pricing page or source.
- Named customers copied from memory or generic logos.
- Feature parity claims without documentation or screenshots.
- Market share numbers without methodology.
- "Leader," "fastest-growing," or "dominant" without definition.
- Claims attributed to unavailable reports.
- Similar wording across competitors that suggests template filling.
- Missing geography, segment, or product scope.

If verification fails, remove the claim or mark it unknown.

---

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Better Practice |
|---|---|---|
| Treating SEO comparison pages as facts | They are often biased lead-generation content | Use as leads only |
| Comparing list price to estimated net price | Creates false pricing gaps | Normalize price basis |
| Overfilling every matrix cell | Encourages invention | Use unknown with confidence notes |
| Ignoring substitutes | Misses real buying alternatives | Include manual and internal-build options |
| Mixing segments | Compares enterprise and SMB competitors incorrectly | Define target segment upfront |
| Citing old product pages | Product claims change quickly | Record access date and recency |
| Equating messaging with capability | Claims may exceed product reality | Seek docs, demos, reviews, or customer proof |

## Worked Example

Question: How should a new compliance workflow product position against competitors in US mid-market clinics?

Roster:

- Direct: vendors with clinic compliance workflow pages and healthcare-specific case studies.
- Adjacent: horizontal GRC platforms that sell to healthcare but are not clinic-specific.
- Substitutes: spreadsheets, consultants, and modules inside existing practice management systems.

Evidence table excerpt:

| Competitor | Pricing | Positioning | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Competitor A | Public pricing page lists $X per user/month | "Healthcare compliance automation" on homepage | High |
| Competitor B | Pricing not disclosed | Case study names regional clinic network | Medium |
| Substitute | Internal spreadsheet process | Cited in customer interviews or client discovery | Medium |

Synthesis:

- The category appears crowded around generic compliance automation, but fewer competitors show clinic-specific workflows with implementation speed evidence.
- Pricing transparency is limited, so do not claim the client can undercut all competitors.
- Recommended positioning should emphasize clinic-specific time-to-value only if product evidence and customer proof support that claim.
