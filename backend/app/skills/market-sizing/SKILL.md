# Market Sizing

Estimate market opportunity with explicit definitions, transparent math, triangulated sources, and defensible ranges. A useful market size is not just a large number; it is a decision-grade estimate tied to a customer definition, purchase occasion, geography, period, and reachable business model.

---

## Overview

Market sizing translates demand into a quantified opportunity. The work should make clear what is being counted, who is counted, where the market exists, when the revenue occurs, and how much of it the client can realistically access.

The core principle: never present a market figure unless the audience can trace the unit logic from source evidence to final estimate.

Use ranges by default. A precise unsupported TAM is weaker than a transparent low/base/high estimate with assumptions the client can challenge.

---

## When to Use

Use market sizing when the engagement requires:

- Market entry attractiveness assessment.
- Growth opportunity prioritization across segments or geographies.
- Investor, acquisition, or partnership diligence.
- Product launch demand estimation.
- Sales capacity or revenue planning.
- Strategic comparison of adjacent markets.
- Sanity-checking analyst, vendor, or management market claims.

Do not use market sizing as a substitute for customer validation. A large market does not prove willingness to buy, channel access, competitive advantage, or profitable unit economics.

## Core Framework

### TAM, SAM, and SOM

| Layer | Definition | Decision Use |
|---|---|---|
| TAM | Total addressable market if every theoretically relevant customer bought the category | How large is the broad opportunity? |
| SAM | Serviceable available market within the chosen product scope, geography, channel, and regulatory boundary | What part can this offer actually serve? |
| SOM | Serviceable obtainable market based on realistic share capture over a defined period | What revenue can the client plausibly win? |

Use these layers in sequence. Do not jump from TAM to revenue forecast without narrowing to SAM and SOM.

### Required Definition Fields

Every estimate must state:

- **Customer or account unit**: people, households, companies, facilities, devices, orders, subscriptions, seats, or transactions.
- **Revenue unit**: annual spend, one-time purchase, recurring subscription, transaction value, take rate, or margin pool.
- **Geography**: country, region, city, global, or addressable operating footprint.
- **Period**: current year, forecast year, annual run-rate, multi-year cumulative, or CAGR window.
- **Currency**: currency and whether figures are nominal, real, gross revenue, net revenue, GMV, or margin.
- **Scope exclusions**: segments, products, channels, or use cases intentionally excluded.

## Workflow

### Step 1: Frame the Market

Write the market question in one sentence:

"What is the annual revenue opportunity for [offering] sold to [customer] in [geography] during [period]?"

Then define the market boundary. If the boundary is ambiguous, create two versions: a narrow operating market and a broader strategic market.

### Step 2: Build a Top-Down View

Start from an external aggregate source such as industry revenue, government statistics, category spend, installed base, or analyst estimates.

Adjust top-down figures for:

- Geographic coverage.
- Product scope fit.
- Customer segment fit.
- Channel availability.
- Regulation or reimbursement limits.
- Revenue definition differences such as gross bookings vs. net revenue.

### Step 3: Build a Bottom-Up View

Start from countable units and spend per unit.

Common bottom-up structures:

- Accounts x penetration x average contract value.
- Users x adoption x annual revenue per user.
- Transactions x frequency x average order value x take rate.
- Facilities x eligible use cases x annual spend per facility.
- Installed base x replacement cycle x price per replacement.

### Step 4: Triangulate

Compare top-down, bottom-up, and comparable-market views. The goal is not to average blindly. The goal is to explain why estimates differ and select the most credible range.

If top-down and bottom-up outputs differ by more than 2x, do not hide the gap. Reconcile definitions, source years, geography, units, and price assumptions.

### Step 5: Convert to SAM and SOM

Move from broad demand to reachable demand:

- TAM: all plausible demand in the defined category.
- SAM: demand reachable with the selected product, geography, and channel.
- SOM: share capture the client can win within the planning period.

SOM should reflect sales capacity, buyer switching friction, competitive response, regulatory approval, procurement cycles, and customer trust barriers.

### Step 6: Sensitize the Answer

Build low/base/high cases around the assumptions that matter most. Usually these are eligible unit count, adoption or penetration, price, frequency, and achievable share.

Show the range and the driver responsible for most variance. If the recommendation changes across the range, call that out.

## Evidence Requirements

Every material market figure needs a source or a clearly labeled calculation. Evidence should include:

- Source name, URL if available, publication date, and accessed date when relevant.
- Exact figure used and whether it is quoted, converted, or calculated.
- Definitions from the source, especially geography, revenue basis, and category scope.
- Any extrapolation method, such as applying a regional share or CAGR.
- Confidence level for each major assumption.

Reject unsupported market figures when:

- No source or calculation path is provided.
- The source does not define the market.
- The figure mixes TAM, SAM, revenue, GMV, or profit pool without explanation.
- The geography or period is missing.
- The number appears copied from a search snippet or vendor claim with no primary support.
- The estimate is implausible against population, account count, budget, or spending benchmarks.

---

## Sanity Checks

Use at least three checks before presenting the estimate:

- **Population check**: Does implied penetration exceed the eligible population?
- **Budget check**: Does implied spend fit customer budgets or category spend?
- **Share check**: Does SOM imply a plausible share gain versus incumbents?
- **Price check**: Does ARPU or average order value match observed pricing?
- **Growth check**: Does implied CAGR exceed what capacity, adoption, or regulation can support?
- **Benchmark check**: Does the estimate align with comparable markets or company revenues?

If a sanity check fails, revise the estimate or explicitly flag the estimate as unreliable.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Better Practice |
|---|---|---|
| Calling a broad category TAM the client opportunity | Overstates reachable revenue | Narrow to SAM and SOM |
| Mixing users, accounts, and transactions | Creates double counting | Pick one unit logic per calculation |
| Using one analyst number unchallenged | Hides definition mismatch | Triangulate with bottom-up math |
| Ignoring period and currency | Makes figures incomparable | State year, currency, and revenue basis |
| Averaging inconsistent estimates | Produces false precision | Reconcile definitions first |
| Treating GMV as revenue | Inflates platform economics | State GMV, net revenue, and take rate separately |
| Presenting a point estimate only | Hides uncertainty | Use low/base/high ranges |

## Worked Example

Question: What is the annual SAM for a B2B compliance software product sold to mid-market clinics in the United States in 2026?

Definition:

- Unit: clinic locations.
- Geography: United States.
- Period: 2026 annual recurring revenue.
- Revenue basis: software subscription ARR in USD.
- Exclusions: hospitals, solo practices, and non-clinical administrative offices.

Bottom-up estimate:

- 45,000 relevant clinic locations.
- 60% have compliance complexity requiring dedicated software.
- $4,000 to $8,000 annual subscription spend per eligible location.
- SAM range: 45,000 x 60% x $4,000 to $8,000 = $108M to $216M ARR.

Top-down check:

- External category spend suggests $1.2B US healthcare compliance software revenue.
- Mid-market clinics represent an estimated 10% to 15% of category spend.
- Top-down SAM range: $120M to $180M.

Synthesis:

- Use $120M to $200M as the decision range after triangulation.
- State that SOM is lower: if the client can reach 5% to 8% of SAM in five years, obtainable ARR is $6M to $16M.
- Flag key sensitivities: eligible clinic count, actual compliance software penetration, and achievable channel reach.
